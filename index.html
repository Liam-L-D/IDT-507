Information Cultures and Communication Technologies

This project examines four major stages of information culture — oral, print, electronic, and digital and connects them to communication theory in order to better understand how people create, share, and use information across time.



Project Overview

This project examines four major forms of information culture: oral culture, print culture, electronic culture, and digital culture. Each stage represents a distinct mode of communication, shaping how information is created, transmitted, and preserved within society.

To analyze these transformations, this project applies key communication and technology theories, including affordance theory, diffusion of innovations, and practice theory. Each cultural stage is examined through a specific theoretical lens to better understand how technological characteristics influence human behavior, adoption patterns, and everyday communication practices.

By connecting historical developments with theoretical frameworks, this project highlights how evolving information technologies continuously reshape human interaction and knowledge systems.

Oral storytelling traditions emphasize memory, repetition, and communal participation.

1. Oral Culture

Definition

Oral culture refers to communication systems in which knowledge is transmitted through spoken language, storytelling, and memory rather than written or digital media. As discussed by Walter J. Ong in Orality and Literacy [1], oral cultures rely on sound, repetition, and communal participation to preserve and transmit knowledge.

Theory Focus: Affordance

From the perspective of affordance theory, oral communication offers immediacy, flexibility, and emotional expression. Speakers can adapt their message based on audience feedback in real time, enabling dynamic interaction. As Ong explains, oral communication is highly participatory and situational, relying on memory and direct human interaction rather than external storage [1]. However, the absence of written records limits long-term preservation and makes information dependent on human memory.

Example

Examples include storytelling traditions, religious teachings, public speaking, and indigenous oral narratives that preserve cultural identity across generations. For instance, many indigenous communities use oral storytelling to pass down history and cultural knowledge, emphasizing repetition and collective memory (see Oral Tradition, Wikipedia) [5].

My Analysis

From my perspective, oral culture creates strong interpersonal connections and allows communication to be flexible and responsive to context. Compared to later stages, it supports more direct human interaction, but is less reliable for preserving information accurately over time. As messages are repeatedly transmitted, they may change or evolve, leading to variation across generations.

Print culture expanded access to knowledge through reproducible and widely distributed texts.

2. Print Culture

Definition

Print culture emerged with the invention of the printing press and includes books, newspapers, and other printed materials that allow information to be reproduced and distributed widely. As Elizabeth L. Eisenstein explains in The Printing Press as an Agent of Change [2], print transformed communication by stabilizing texts and enabling the wider circulation of knowledge.

Theory Focus: Diffusion of Innovations

The spread of print technology can be understood through diffusion of innovations theory, which explains how new technologies are adopted over time. The printing press allowed information to be reproduced consistently and distributed to larger audiences, accelerating the spread of literacy, education, and knowledge. As Eisenstein suggests, print played a key role in shaping social and intellectual change by making information more stable and accessible [2].

Example

Key examples of print culture include the printing press, newspapers, textbooks, pamphlets, and academic journals. For example, the invention of the printing press enabled the mass production of books and the spread of ideas such as religious reform and scientific knowledge (see Printing Press, Wikipedia) [6].

My Analysis

From my perspective, print culture makes information more stable and reliable compared to oral culture because ideas can be recorded and preserved over time. It supports long-term storage and wide distribution of knowledge, but it also depends on literacy and physical access to printed materials. Compared to oral culture, print expands the reach of communication while still limiting participation to those who can read and access texts.

Electronic media enabled faster communication and laid the foundation for interactive media environments.

3. Electronic Culture

Definition

Electronic culture includes communication technologies such as the telegraph, telephone, radio, and television. These systems increased the speed and reach of information transmission and introduced new forms of mass communication, connecting large audiences across distance.

Theory Focus

Electronic culture can be understood through Jenkins’ concept of convergence culture, which explains how different media platforms begin to interact and overlap. As technologies evolve, communication is no longer limited to a single medium, but involves the integration of multiple media forms that reshape how people access and share information [3].

Example

Examples of electronic culture include radio broadcasts, television programs, and telephone communication. These technologies allowed information to reach large audiences quickly and simultaneously, shaping modern mass media systems (see Electronic media, Wikipedia) [7].

My Analysis

From my perspective, electronic culture represents a transition between traditional and digital communication. Compared to print culture, it allows faster and broader distribution of information. However, communication is still mostly one-directional, with audiences primarily receiving content. This stage sets the foundation for the more interactive and participatory nature of digital culture.

Digital culture enables networked, interactive, and global communication through connected digital systems.

4. Digital Culture

Definition

Digital culture is shaped by computers, the internet, and mobile technologies that allow people to communicate and access information instantly. It is defined by networked, interactive, and global forms of communication. As Manuel Castells explains in The Rise of the Network Society [4], digital communication is organized through networks that connect individuals, institutions, and information across the world.

Theory Focus

Digital culture can be understood through Castells’ concept of the network society, which emphasizes how information flows through interconnected global networks. In this environment, communication is no longer limited by physical location, as digital systems allow people to interact, share ideas, and participate in information exchange across time and space [4].

Example

Common examples of digital culture include social media platforms, websites, online learning systems, and artificial intelligence tools. These technologies allow users to communicate, collaborate, and create content in real time while participating in global networks (see Digital media, Wikipedia) [8].

My Analysis

From my perspective, digital culture is different from earlier stages because it makes communication faster, more interactive, and more globally connected. People are no longer only readers or viewers, but also active participants who produce and share information. Compared to electronic culture, digital communication is more networked and flexible, but it also creates challenges such as misinformation, information overload, and concerns about the reliability of online content.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the shift from oral to digital culture shows how communication technologies continuously transform the ways people store, distribute, and interpret information. Each stage: oral, print, electronic, and digital, which offers different strengths and limitations, shaping how individuals interact with information in everyday life.

Through the application of communication theories such as affordance theory, diffusion of innovations, and practice theory, this project demonstrates that technology is not only a tool for delivering information, but also a force that influences human behavior, social practices, and knowledge systems. As communication evolves, people adapt their habits and expectations based on the possibilities and constraints of each technology.

Looking ahead, digital culture may continue to evolve into new forms of interaction, including more direct communication between humans and artificial intelligence systems. This suggests that communication is no longer limited to human-to-human exchange, but may increasingly involve human – AI interaction as part of everyday information practices. While this creates new opportunities for efficiency and creativity, it also raises important questions about trust, authorship, and the role of technology in shaping human communication.
